What is Berghain?

Think of it as the bouncer at the velvet rope, and your backend as the party on the other side.

Berghain is a stateless bot-gate that sits in front of a website. It is implemented as an HAProxy SPOE agent (Stream Processing Offload Engine) written in Go. Before a request is allowed through to the real application, Berghain checks whether the visitor is carrying a valid “clearance” — and if not, it briefly holds the request and shows a short verification screen instead.

The goal is simple: keep automated abuse out while letting real browsers in with as little friction as possible. Legitimate visitors usually pass through in a few seconds and rarely see the screen again, because their clearance is remembered by their own browser (in a cookie), not by a server-side database.

Stateless by design

There is no session store to look you up in. Your clearance travels with you in a single signed cookie, so Berghain can scale horizontally and forgets you the moment the cookie expires.

Runs at the edge

It plugs into HAProxy, a battle-tested load balancer, so the check happens before traffic reaches your application — no code changes to the protected site are required.

Why bot protection?

The modern web is crawled relentlessly. Scrapers, credential-stuffing scripts, content thieves and aggressive AI-training crawlers can generate far more traffic than real people, driving up costs and degrading the experience for everyone else.

  • Stop automated scraping of content, prices and personal data.
  • Blunt credential-stuffing and account-takeover attempts before they hit login forms.
  • Shield fragile or expensive endpoints from floods of automated requests.
  • Keep infrastructure costs down by turning away traffic that was never a customer.

Berghain asks every visitor to prove they are running a real, capable browser. That is cheap for a genuine person and their browser, but expensive to fake at scale for a bot farm.

Privacy

Berghain is built to verify you without building a profile of you.

One signed clearance cookie

Your authorization lives entirely in a single signed berghain cookie. It is an HMAC computed over the host you are visiting, your IP address, the challenge level and an expiry time. If any of those change, the cookie no longer validates — and that is the whole mechanism.

No server-side store

Because verification is stateless, there is no database of visitors, sessions or fingerprints to query, leak or subpoena. The server only re-computes the signature and compares it. Nothing about your visit is retained after the cookie expires.

Your IP is bound, not stored

Your IP address is mixed into the cookie's signature so a clearance cannot be copied to another machine. It is bound cryptographically, not saved in plaintext as a record of where you were.

No third-party CDN

The verification screen and any proof-of-work run in your own browser and are served from the same site you are visiting. There are no external trackers, fonts or scripts loaded from someone else's servers — and this help page follows the same rule.

The optional proof-of-work challenge is computed client-side, in your browser. The server hands out a small puzzle and only checks the answer; it never watches how you move your mouse or type.

Blocked? Troubleshooting

If you keep seeing the “Request on Hold” screen and the verification will not complete, it usually means your browser could not run or store the check. Work through the list below — most cases are fixed by one of the first three items.

1. Update your browser

Very old browsers may lack the features the challenge relies on. Install the latest version of your browser and try again.

2. Enable JavaScript

The verification runs a small piece of JavaScript. If scripts are disabled globally or for this site, the check can never finish. Allow JavaScript and reload.

3. Enable cookies

Your clearance is stored in a cookie. If cookies are blocked, you will be sent back to the verification screen on every request. Allow cookies for the site and reload.

4. Disable aggressive extensions

Some privacy or script-blocking extensions interfere with the challenge. Temporarily disable them (see Browser extensions) and reload.

5. Try another browser

If the verification still repeats, try a different, mainstream browser to confirm whether the problem is your current setup.

6. Still stuck?

If the verification still repeats on an up-to-date, mainstream browser, report an issue with your browser and operating system so an operator can investigate.

Browser compatibility

The verification needs a modern browser with JavaScript and cookies enabled. The proof-of-work step hashes with the Web Crypto API when the site is served over HTTPS, and falls back to a bundled JavaScript SHA-256 implementation on plain HTTP, so it does not depend on a secure context to work.

Browser extensions

Most extensions are harmless. A few — specifically strict script blockers and canvas / fingerprint blockers — can break the JavaScript challenge, because they remove exactly the browser capabilities the check depends on.

Rule of thumb: if the verification loops forever, temporarily disable script- and canvas-blocking extensions, reload, and re-enable them once you are through.

Open Source

Berghain is open source. Nothing about the door policy is a secret.

Because Berghain relies on cryptography (an HMAC-signed cookie) rather than on hiding how it works, the whole thing can be public. You can read the code, audit exactly what data the cookie contains, verify the privacy claims on this page for yourself, run it on your own infrastructure, and contribute improvements.

Network & client restrictions

What a visitor must satisfy depends on how the operator has configured Berghain and the surrounding HAProxy setup. The list below separates what Berghain itself enforces today from optional measures an operator may add. Expand each item to learn more — no JavaScript required.

Implemented today

Enforced by Berghain

JavaScript is required

The verification screen runs a small script to complete the challenge and obtain your clearance cookie. With JavaScript disabled, the check cannot proceed. This is a core part of how Berghain distinguishes real browsers from simple scripts.

Cookies are required

Your clearance is a single signed cookie. If your browser will not store it, every request looks unverified and you are returned to the challenge. Cookies must be enabled for the protected site.

Progressive friction (challenge duration & countdown)

Operators define one or more levels, each with its own duration and, optionally, a short countdown before the check completes (configurable from 0 to 9 seconds). Higher levels can impose a longer or more visible wait, so protection can escalate for riskier traffic while ordinary visitors get the lightest touch.

Proof-of-work challenge

A level can require a proof-of-work: your browser must find an input whose hash begins with a run of zero bits. It is deliberately cheap for one genuine visitor but costly to repeat across a bot farm. The computation runs entirely in your browser — the server only hands out the puzzle and checks the answer.

Planned / operator-optional

May be enabled by the operator

These are not core Berghain guarantees. They describe measures an operator may layer on in HAProxy or via an optional feed. Whether any apply to you depends entirely on the site you are visiting.
IP reputation (CrowdSec)

An operator may consult a community threat feed such as CrowdSec and apply extra friction, or a block, to addresses with a poor reputation.

VPN & Tor exit handling

Traffic arriving from known VPN endpoints or Tor exit nodes may be treated with more caution, since these are frequently used to mask automated abuse.

Datacenter / ASN blocking

Requests originating from hosting-provider and datacenter networks (by ASN) may face stricter handling, because ordinary visitors rarely browse from server ranges.

Cloudflare WARP

Operators may choose how to treat traffic relayed through Cloudflare WARP, either allowing it or applying additional checks.

Apple Private Relay

Connections coming through Apple Private Relay may be handled with a dedicated policy so that legitimate iCloud+ users are not unduly penalized.

AI-browser handling

Automated “AI browsers” and agentic crawlers may be identified and routed to stricter challenges or a dedicated tarpit at the operator's discretion.

Rate limiting

Operators commonly add request rate limits in HAProxy so that even verified clients cannot flood an endpoint. The example also applies a flat, expiring ban after too many challenge submissions; the verification screen shows the remaining time. Limits and thresholds are set per deployment.

Report an issue

If you believe you are being blocked by mistake, or the verification will not complete after working through the troubleshooting steps, please open an issue so an operator can help.

What to include

Tell us your browser and version, your operating system, the address you were trying to reach and what happened. A screenshot of the verification screen helps too. The more detail you provide, the faster an operator can reproduce and fix the problem.
